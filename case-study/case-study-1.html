Navigation & IA Redesign

Simplified navigation to reduce friction and improve user flow.

User Experience ◇ Information Architecture ◇ 2025

Redesigned the menu structure to reduce friction, improve navigation clarity, and guide users through a simplified, goal-focused experience.

Role

UI UX Designer

Timeframe

~1 months

Tools

Figma, Fig Jam

Team

Sapna (UI/UX Designer), Sreelakshmi (UI/UX Designer), Aayushi (Co-Founder of First Contact and PM), Shrinkhla (UI/UX Designer)

Context

During my UX internship, I was tasked with improving the usability and accessibility of the website’s main navigation. The problem: when users landed on the site, the menu was cluttered and poorly grouped. Even after logging in, they saw no change—just the same outdated menu with a “Logout” option. This confused users and made navigation difficult.

The goal was to redesign the information architecture to provide a more intuitive, task-focused navigation experience, especially during what we called the user's “First Contact” after login.

What's FirstConatct.lgbtq?

Problem

The website's menu was cluttered and unclear. When users landed on the site, they struggled to find what they were looking for due to poorly grouped menu items. Even after logging in, the interface remained almost unchanged—users saw the same menu with just a "Logout" button added, making them question whether their login was successful.

Key Issues:
• No distinction between guest and member navigation.
• Poor menu categorization and hierarchy.
• Low usability and accessibility.
• Confusing "First Contact" after login.

Solution

We delivered a restructured, responsive menu experience:
• Non-logged-in users now see a simplified navigation, focused on exploration.
• Menu items are now grouped logically and aligned with user expectations.
• A better first impression after login, reducing confusion and improving navigation confidence.

Outcome

This internship project provided me with hands-on training in redesigning user interaction with an information platform’s structure. I learned to reconcile user requirements and technical constraints—in keeping the experience clear, flexible, and engaging—together, by means of testing and iteration.

What we learned:
Design thrives based on flexibility, cross-functional collaboration, and ongoing iteration.

My personal key takeaway:
Designing a better "First Contact" isn't just about clean navigation—it’s about helping users feel not only oriented, but that this space belongs to them. The interface should welcome them, guide them, and reinforce that they’re in the right place, with access to what matters most to them.

STEP TWO
Brainstorm and Ideate
My design goals: Clearly separate experiences for guests and members, Group menu items logically for better usability, Make the logged-in experience feel purposeful and structured, Improve accessibility and overall user flow.

After mapping out the user journey, we brainstormed and explored:

  • Dual interface paths: one for guests, one for logged-in users.
  • Dashboard-style navigation for members.
  • A top-level menu structure based on common tasks and mental models.

We also explored “first-contact clarity”: what users expect to see immediately after logging in.
