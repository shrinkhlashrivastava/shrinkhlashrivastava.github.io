Navigation & IA Redesign

Simplified navigation to reduce friction and improve user flow.

User Experience ◇ Information Architecture ◇ 2025

Redesigned the menu structure to reduce friction, improve navigation clarity, and guide users through a simplified, goal-focused experience.

Role

UI UX Designer

Timeframe

~1 months

Tools

Figma, Fig Jam

Team

Sapna (UI/UX Designer), Sreelakshmi (UI/UX Designer), Aayushi (Co-Founder of First Contact and PM), Shrinkhla (UI/UX Designer)

Context

I stepped in as FC’s core UI/UX designer and teamed up with a fellow designer to reimagine our overlooked Search Member page. I set the design vision, sketched and prototyped the crucial bits, and made sure every element from filters to profile cards felt succinct, consistent, and unmistakably on‑brand. Working hand in hand with product, engineering, and community, we aimed for one seamless experience across web, mobile, and metaverse platforms.

Problem

Our Search Member page was practically invisible: a disconnected layout, confusing filters, and jumbled member cards. Few noticed it and those who did had lost faith. It was clunky, underused, and begging for a thoughtful overhaul.

Solution

We rebuilt the experience with one goal: clarity. Filters got a full reset: collapsible sections, smart groupings, and labels that actually meant something. They went from being ignored to feeling intuitive, fast, almost effortless.

The member cards followed suit. I designed flexible templates that worked whether a profile had two data points or twenty, always staying clean and consistent.

We nearly split the page into tabs but opted for a single, seamless view. That choice kept users in one flow, whether they were searching, browsing, or just exploring. With live result counts, snap-in chips, and real-time feedback, everything clicked into place on-brand and in sync with the rest of FC.

Outcome

The redesign pulled everything into a smooth, cohesive experience. Filters were finally clear easy to find, easier to use. With live result feedback and snap-in chips, users got immediate clarity, whether they were searching by name or browsing by traits.

Member cards became consistent, clean, and flexible, ready to represent every type of user. Visually and functionally, the page now felt like a natural part of the FC ecosystem.

Most importantly, people noticed. A feature once buried and ignored started getting real traction. What was once a dead-end became a tool users trusted and used. Thoughtful design didn’t just improve the UI, it brought the feature back to life

STEP ONE
Identified the blockers
We started by pinpointing the core issue: filters. They were vague, unstable, and often ignored. Users didn’t know how to use them—and many didn’t bother trying. Fixing this became our top priority.



STEP TWO
Rebuild the filters
I redesigned the filter panel to be clear and actionable—explicit labels, grouped logic, while Pratham worked on collapsible sections that made the interface feel lighter and more approachable. We tested functionality early with engineers to keep it practical and scalable.



STEP THREE
Structured the member cards
The existing member cards were inconsistent and unstructured. I created a flexible layout system that worked across all profiles—minimal or dense—keeping the design clean, readable, and on-brand.



STEP FOUR
Simplified the overall flow
At one point, we debated splitting the experience into tabs. But a quick gut-check with engineering confirmed we could meet all use cases in a single view. That decision helped us maintain a seamless, friction-free experience.



STEP FIVE
Iteration
I worked independently on the member cards. Initially, they existed solely as placeholders in an empty form with no substance. I populated each card with information, organizing elements to correspond to the sorts of input users would provide. By trying both minimal and elaborate layouts, I made certain that every profile was consistent in appearance, whether much data existed or little.



Reflection
This project reminded me how powerful early, cross‑team feedback can be. By asking engineers the right questions up front, we kept things simple before complexity crept in. Small changes, better filters, live counts rebuilt user trust and revived a feature that had been gathering dust. Thoughtful, clear UX didn’t just tidy things up; it brought our Search Member page back to life.
